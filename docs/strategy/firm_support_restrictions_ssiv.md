# Firm-Support Restrictions in the AR-Test SSIV

## Question

The endogenous variable in the AR test is the vector of sector employment shares $s_{jmt} = n_{jmt} / n_{mt}$, with $n_{mt} = \sum_j n_{jmt}$ aggregating RAIS firms. Exposure weights $w_{jm,\tau}$ are constructed over the four-year pre-election window $\tau \in [e(t){-}4, e(t){-}1]$. The choice question is which firms enter the construction of $n_{jmt}$ and $w_{jm,\tau}$: (A) all firms present each year (fully unbalanced), (B) only firms active in the pre-election window (baseline-frozen on the firm dimension), or (C) firms present in $\tau$ and all post-election $t$ (balanced panel). The trade-off is between contamination of the endogenous variable by post-shock entry/exit and survivorship bias / lost relevance from restricting the support.

## What the literature does

Standard practice in the canonical SSIV literature is to predetermine the *exposure weights* (the shares used to build the instrument) but to construct the *endogenous outcome variable* on the contemporaneous, fully unbalanced universe. The two are sharply distinguished. In Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013), the regional industry mix used to compute import exposure is taken from pre-period employment (1990 or 1980 Census base-year shares), but the dependent variable — change in manufacturing employment between $t$ and $t{+}10$ — is computed on the contemporaneous County Business Patterns and Census/ACS populations with no balanced-firm restriction. Dix-Carneiro and Kovak (2017) make the same separation explicit for the Brazil setting most directly comparable to ours: their regional industry mix $\lambda_{ri}$ is computed from the 1991 Census specifically because "it provides a less aggregate industry definition than what is available in RAIS, and the Census allows calculation of weights that are representative of overall employment, rather than just formal employment," but the outcomes (formal wages, formal employment) are measured in RAIS year-by-year over 1986–2010 on the contemporaneous formal-sector population. Bustos, Caprettini, and Ponticelli (2016) use pre-period agricultural shares for exposure and contemporaneous outcomes.

The Brazil/RAIS literature follows the same template. Costa, Garred, and Pessoa (2016) construct microregion-level exposure to the China shock from 2000 Census base-year employment shares — precisely because RAIS does not cover the informal sector that participates in the response — and measure outcomes (wages, formalization) over 2000–2010 on the contemporaneous RAIS panel. Felix (2022) exploits the 1990–1994 cross-sector variation in tariff cuts; the local-labor-market exposure uses pre-reform industry shares as the share component, with outcomes on contemporaneous RAIS firms. Neither paper restricts the outcome panel to baseline-period firms. The recent Brazil-RAIS papers most often cited as a benchmark for credit-allocation work — Ponticelli and Alencar (2016) — do not use a shift-share design at all; they instrument via state-level judicial-organization laws and within-state cross-district variation in court congestion, so their convention does not transfer to the SSIV setting. The Dix-Carneiro, Pessoa, Reyes-Heroles, and Traiberman (2023, QJE) globalization paper is structural rather than shift-share and is silent on firm-support choice. Where the closest comparable papers have a stated convention, it is the ADH/Dix-Carneiro–Kovak split: freeze the share basis at a pre-period reference, leave the outcome panel unbalanced.

The two canonical identification frameworks differ on what they require of the *shares* but agree on the role of the outcome. Goldsmith-Pinkham, Sorkin, and Swift (2020) require that the exposure shares be exogenous conditional on controls — their decomposition treats each pre-period share as an instrument and asks whether it is uncorrelated with the structural error. Here "predetermined" is an operational convention for implementing share exogeneity, not the identifying assumption itself: a predetermined-but-anticipated share would still violate $E[\varepsilon_\ell s_{\ell k}] = 0$. Borusyak, Hull, and Jaravel (2022) relax share exogeneity in favor of quasi-random assignment of shocks (Assumption 1: $E[g_n \mid \bar\varepsilon, s] = \mu$). The "endogenous shares are admissible" reading often associated with BHJ is too strong: the conditional-mean assumption is *conditional on shares*, so a share vector whose firm support is itself selected on the outcome contaminates the conditioning. What BHJ buys is robustness to share endogeneity *given* shock orthogonality, not a license for arbitrary share construction. With panel data and unit fixed effects, BHJ §4.3 also notes that "unit fixed effects only isolate variation in shocks over time when exposure shares are time-invariant," and BHJ §4.4 discusses the incomplete-shares case in which the sum of exposure weights must be controlled. Both points argue for stabilising the *exposure weights*, not the firm panel underlying the endogenous variable.

Adão, Kolesár, and Morales (2019) treat shares as fixed regressors and randomize over shocks; their concern is correlated *effective shocks* across regions sharing share structure, not firm-support selection per se. The implication for inference is the cluster-robust standard error correction; the implication for firm-support choice is indirect. The relevant diagnostic for whether share construction is loading on a non-representative subset of the firm population is the Rotemberg-weight concentration check in GPSS §4.2. The Borusyak-Hull-Jaravel JEP 2025 practical guide reinforces this division of labor: their checklist recommends pre-trend tests on the high-Rotemberg-weight industries and sensitivity to the choice of base period for share construction, and does not advocate freezing the firm panel for the outcome.

A second methodological layer applies because the test of interest is an Anderson-Rubin test. Firm-support choice has downstream consequences for AR validity and power that the SSIV identification literature does not address directly. Andrews, Stock, and Sun (2019) note that AR is fully robust under weak instruments and should be reported regardless of the first-stage $F$, but its power depends on first-stage signal: a frozen-firm panel that drops politically connected post-election entrants attenuates first-stage relevance and shifts AR power toward the null. Dufour (1997) shows that any valid confidence set for a parameter that can be locally unidentified must be unbounded with positive probability; AR confidence sets are by construction in this class. A frozen specification that further weakens first-stage signal raises the probability of unbounded AR confidence regions in finite samples. Moreira's (2003) CLR test is similarity-preserving and has better power than AR against most local alternatives in just-identified IV; with our over-identified $K$-instrument setup, AR remains the natural pivot, but the choice of firm panel affects the effective $K$ that determines AR's behavior. Mikusheva and Sun (2022) develop the jackknifed AR test for the many-weak-instruments regime; the relevance is that as $K$ grows (e.g., with finer sector partitions), choices that further weaken the first stage push us toward their regime. None of these points overturn the basic ADH/DK convention, but they raise the cost of an unnecessarily conservative frozen-firm specification.

## Recommendation

Adopt a hybrid: freeze the firm support in the exposure weights $w_{jm,\tau}$ (compute $w_{jm,\tau}$ over firms active in the four-year pre-election window only), but compute the endogenous variable $s_{jmt}$ on the fully unbalanced contemporaneous RAIS universe. This is option (B) restricted to the instrument and option (A) for the endogenous variable. The recommendation maps onto the literature: it implements GPSS's predetermined-share requirement and BHJ's "shocks orthogonal to shares conditional on shares" framework for the instrument side, while letting the endogenous share vector reflect the actual sectoral composition that produces municipal GDP — the object the AR null is testing.

The reasoning has three pillars.

(i) The economic claim of the paper is about sectoral-composition optimality of the realised local economy conditional on the volume of activity. The endogenous variable must therefore reflect the composition firms actually produce, including post-election entrants and exiters whose presence (or absence) is itself the channel through which the political shock can affect output. Excluding endogenous entry from $s_{jmt}$ would mechanically replace the test of $H_0: \beta = 0$ on the realised share vector with a test on a counterfactual fixed-firm share vector that has no direct interpretation in the structural model. The share vector enters the AR test not as an exogenous regressor but as the *regressor under test* — the parameter being inverted on. The share-exogeneity assumptions in GPSS govern *instruments*; the endogeneity of $s_{jmt}$ as a regressor is exactly what the IV machinery is built to handle.

(ii) On the instrument side, freezing firm support over $[e(t){-}4, e(t){-}1]$ is conceptually correct: $w_{jm,\tau}$ is meant to capture pre-existing political exposure of the local economy, and any post-election change in the firm roster is downstream of the shock the instrument is supposed to predict. This is the GPSS predetermined-share logic.

(iii) BHJ identification is permissive in the sense that endogeneity in the shares is reabsorbed when shocks are conditionally mean-independent of unobservables. The frozen-instrument / unbalanced-outcome split keeps both identification routes available: if firm-roster churn during the pre-election window is itself politically driven, identification can rest on quasi-random shock assignment (national party turnover) rather than share exogeneity. Option (C), the fully balanced panel, would attenuate first-stage relevance dramatically, push the design toward the Mikusheva-Sun many-weak-instruments regime, and induce the kind of non-representative-share-vector selection that the GPSS Rotemberg-weight diagnostic is designed to detect.

Three operational notes attach to the hybrid. First, $w_{jm,\tau}$ is constructed on the frozen pre-period firm set with denominator $n^{\text{frozen}}_{m,\tau}$, while $s_{jmt}$ uses the contemporaneous denominator $n_{mt}$. The denominators differ. Per BHJ §4.4, this is an incomplete-shares case: the sum of exposure shares within the frozen baseline does not equal one over the contemporaneous universe. The normalization is to treat $w_{jm,\tau}$ as a share within the frozen baseline (so $\sum_j w_{jm,\tau} = 1$ by construction within that universe) and to include the sum-of-exposure-shares control — equivalently the share of $n_{mt}$ accounted for by frozen-baseline firms — so that variation in the slack between the two denominators does not contaminate the instrument. Second, "firm" in RAIS is the CNPJ. Firm entry and exit conflate genuine birth and death with ownership transfers triggering new CNPJ assignment, re-registration after spells of inactivity, and plant openings under existing parent CNPJs. The entry/exit robustness checks below should be read with this in mind, and one of them (continuing-CNPJ subsample) implicitly cleans for the establishment-vs-firm margin.

Third, the volume control specification has been refined to align with D1's RAIS-only analyzed universe (user 2026-05-13). The primary joint volume term entered in the AR test is $\text{total\_bndes\_real}_{mt}$ — disbursements to RAIS-merged productive firms only — normalized by initial municipal GDP. This makes the volume control conformable with the endogenous-share denominator: both objects are constructed on the same RAIS-productive universe. The split-volume robustness exercise (D3.3 in the firm-support hybrid implementation plan, `journal/plans/2026-05-12_firm_support_hybrid_implementation.md`) decomposes the remaining BNDES disbursement mass into three additional volume terms entered separately: productive-nonRAIS (loans to private firms with productive CNAE codes but no RAIS row in year $t$ — roughly 42% of D3.1's productive aggregate), financial-institution (FI), and public-entity. Loans through other public-credit channels — CEF, Banco do Brasil, Banco do Nordeste, constitutional funds — are out of scope for this design and are registered as a forward A-entry in the blueprint rather than absorbed into the volume control.

## Robustness checks

- Re-run the AR test on a fully balanced panel (option C) and confirm the rejection region is qualitatively stable. Report attrition rate as a diagnostic.
- Re-run with $s_{jmt}$ computed on the same baseline-frozen firm set used to compute $w_{jm,\tau}$ (option B for both) — quantifies how much of the test rests on post-election entry/exit being part of the composition channel.
- Decompose the entry/exit margin: rebuild $s_{jmt}$ separately for (i) continuing CNPJs only, (ii) including only entrants, (iii) including only exiters. Sign-stability of the AR statistic across (i)–(iii) is evidence the composition effect is not entirely driven by one margin (read against the CNPJ caveat above).
- 2002-fixed vs cycle-specific exposure baseline contrast (per project D3) — already in place; re-run on the baseline-frozen-instrument variant.
- Balance test on $w_{jm,\tau}$: regress firm-level pre-election exit on the firm's political-alignment exposure to confirm the pre-period roster is not itself selected by anticipated political turnover.
- Sensitivity to the pre-election window length: replace $[e(t){-}4, e(t){-}1]$ with $[e(t){-}2, e(t){-}1]$ and with $[e(t){-}6, e(t){-}1]$. Stable AR rejection regions across windows rule out share-support timing as the main driver.
- Rotemberg-weight diagnostic per GPSS §4.2: rank the underlying $K$ share-instruments by their Rotemberg weight and report the share of variation carried by the top five. If one or two industries dominate, run the AR test excluding them and verify rejection survives. Pair with pre-trend tests on the high-weight industries.
- Sum-of-exposure-shares control: report results with and without the slack control to verify the incomplete-shares correction is binding.
- First-stage strength across the three options: report first-stage $F$ (effective $F$ per Olea–Pflueger or the AR diagnostic equivalent) for options (A), (B), and (C). Sharp drop in the balanced specification would document the cost of option (C).
- Report the share of $n_{mt}$ accounted for by frozen-baseline firms over time; if this share collapses sharply post-2013, flag that the composition channel may load on a selected subset of municipalities.
- **Split-volume robustness (D3.3, implemented Phase 3).** Replace the single joint volume term with four separately-entered volume controls: productive-RAIS (primary), productive-nonRAIS, financial-institution, and public-entity, each normalized by initial GDP. At the `policy_block` partition, the joint-volume AR F-statistic is 4.37 ($p = 2 \times 10^{-4}$); the split-volume AR F is 4.30 ($p = 2.4 \times 10^{-4}$). At `cnae_section`, joint and split AR F-statistics are 2.05 and 2.03 respectively (both $p \approx 2 \times 10^{-4}$). Rejection regions are qualitatively unchanged. The financial-institution coefficient under the split-volume reduced form is $-0.018$ (significant at 5%), qualitatively matching the Phase 1 OLS estimate of $-0.105$ on the FI channel. The negative loading is consistent with the prior that FI-intermediated BNDES flows route through bank balance sheets rather than into local production capacity, and so do not feed local GDP at the muni level even though they enlarge total disbursements.
- **Pre-trend characterization (B1.6–B1.8 in Phase 1).** Under the corrected $\tau$-baseline GPSS-style pre-trend test, the apparent governor-cycle pre-trend rejection seen in earlier specifications resolves as a specification artifact: governor elections fall mid-mayoral-cycle, and under strict-timing event windows governor instruments clear at $p = 0.16$. The presidential pre-trend is genuine but moderate under strict timing ($p < 0.005$ on $\Delta \log \text{gdp}$ at $K = 19$). The mayor pre-trend is clean ($p = 0.80$). As an AR-side robustness exercise, dropping the high-pre-trend presidential CNAE sections from the instrument set yields a first-stage $F$ of 53.84 with $p < 10^{-7}$ on the AR rejection. Per user decision (2026-05-12) this exhibit is documented but not a gate on the main test.
- **Margin-specific diagnostics at the production partition (C2.1.5).** At the `policy_block` aggregation ($K = 9$ in production; $K = 12$ effective once cycle-specific shocks are unpacked), the GPSS Rotemberg-weight ranking concentrates a top weight of 0.319 on the governor-coalition cycle-specific Infrastructure block; the top-two cumulative weight is 0.561. Drop-top-1 AR yields $F = 3.37$ ($p < 0.001$, still rejecting); drop-top-2 yields $F = 2.72$ ($p < 0.01$, still rejecting). Slack on/off at the production FE moves the F-statistic by $\Delta F = 0.023$, well within the Phase 1 envelope. The $K = 4$ mayor-only sub-design carries a relative non-centrality of roughly 25% of the `cnae_section` MGP benchmark, but a per-restriction F that is 56% higher, indicating that the power loss from coarsening the partition is partly offset by per-instrument signal density.

## Limitations and forward A-entries

Two limitations are tracked as forward A-entries rather than blockers on the current design.

First, RAIS Negativa — the supplementary RAIS file recording firm-years with zero employees — is not present in the current local extract (user confirmed 2026-05-12). Phase 0 audit A0.1 in `journal/plans/2026-05-12_firm_support_hybrid_implementation.md` inventories the gap: Owner-only CNPJs missing from the active RAIS panel account for an upper bound of 7.64% of the firm universe; this is an upper bound rather than a tight equality because some Owner-only firms are non-RAIS-universe (informal sector, MEI). Under the current data the contemporaneous unbalanced denominator coincides with the $n_{\text{employees}} \geq 1$ restriction (Phase 0 A0.2 confirms a zero-employee rate of 0.0000% in the current panel). Acquisition of RAIS Negativa would let the denominator extend to fully zero-employee firm-years and tighten the coverage bound; this is registered as an A-entry, not a blocker.

Second, the Adão-Kolesár-Morales (2019) AKM cluster-robust standard-error correction has been applied here in its one-way (muni) form. The two-way (muni, year) AKM correction is registered as a forward A-entry. At the production partition $K = 9$, two-way clustering widens t-statistics by roughly 50% relative to the one-way correction: the one-way muni AR F of 4.19 falls to 2.09 under two-way clustering. The headline rejection survives at the 5% level under both. The narrow tail mass between one-way and two-way is not large enough to overturn the qualitative conclusion of the test, but full reporting in the methodology PDF (E4.1) will state the two-way figure alongside the headline.
