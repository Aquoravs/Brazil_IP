## Paragraph Architecture

<!-- How paragraphs typically open, build, and close. -->

**Typical paragraph structure:**
Topic sentence (claim or result) ‚Üí evidence with one anchor citation ‚Üí qualification or mechanism ‚Üí forward-link.

**Opening moves the author uses:**
- Direct claim: *"Sectoral reallocation accounts for X% of the GDP response to BNDES exposure."*
- Magnitude-first: *"A one-standard-deviation increase in [exposure] raises [outcome] by 7 pp."*
- Setup-first (rotate in, don't default to): *"Following Goldsmith-Pinkham et al. (2020), we identify‚Ä¶"*

**Closing moves the author uses:**  
- Forward-link to next subsection: *"We return to the exclusion restriction in Section 4."*
- Bounded qualification: *"The estimate is local to municipalities with at least one connected firm."*
- Mechanism pointer: *"This pattern is consistent with [mechanism] but does not rule out [alternative]."*

**Openings the author avoids:**
- "It is well known that‚Ä¶"
- "In what follows, I discuss‚Ä¶" / "The present document proposes‚Ä¶" / "In the present essay‚Ä¶" ‚Äî all meta-commentary about the paper itself
- C.S. Lewis (or other) epigraphs in research-paper introductions
- Four paragraphs of stage-setting before the research question
- Self-deprecating asides ("Daring to express an opinion without carefully studying the problem,‚Ä¶")

## Section Architecture

<!-- Structural patterns specific to this author's papers. -->

### Introduction
- [e.g., opens with a specific puzzle or policy moment, not a literature overview]
- Open with a puzzle, policy moment, or specifying a gap in the literature that the paper will cover.
- **Headline number by paragraph 2.** State the main empirical finding with magnitude and units before page 1 ends.
- **Cap context-setting at 1 paragraph.** Move long literature framing to a separate Background or Related Literature section.
- **Cap contributions at 3.** One primary, two secondary.
- **Roadmap is one short sentence**, not a paragraph. Working-paper and dissertation drafts may keep a longer roadmap; manuscript drafts for top journals use the one-line form.
- **Citation density:** at most 1‚Äì2 anchor citations per sentence in the introduction. Save 2‚Äì3-per-claim density for the lit review section only.

### Empirical Strategy
- [e.g., states the identifying variation before the equation]
- [e.g., assumption-and-threat paragraph per assumption]
- States the identifying variation in prose before any equation.
- Threats-and-responses, one paragraph per assumption.
- Names the test or estimator with attribution: *"We invert the AR test of Anderson & Rubin (1949) following Mikusheva & Sun (2022)."

### Results
- Magnitude and standard error in prose first, then table reference.
- Pair signed results with their counter-results when both exist.
- **Drop hedging on point estimates.** Hedge identification and external validity, not the number itself.

### Conclusion
- [e.g., one policy paragraph, one generalization paragraph, no summary paragraph]
- One paragraph on what the result implies, one on the limitation, one on the policy or generalization. **No summary paragraph**.

## Lexicon

### Approach: variety over consistency for verbs

1. When the same verb is referenced multiple times in a section, **rotate among 2‚Äì3 synonyms** rather than reusing the same word. (E.g., for "increase": *raises, lifts, expands, drives up, pushes,* depending on register.)
2. When introducing a connector (Likewise, Moreover, Similarly, Consequently, Thus, Hence, Therefore, In turn, By contrast, Conversely, Meanwhile), **vary across paragraphs**.
3. When a precise technical term exists, **use it instead of a hedge.** "Sectoral composition shifts" beats "non-trivial reallocation."

### Watch-words (used heavily in the corpus ‚Äî vary or replace)

| Overused | Reach for instead |
|---|---|
| `non-trivial` | `large`, `material`, `economically meaningful`, `sizable`, or a number |
| `Likewise` | `Similarly`, `In the same vein`, `By the same token`, `Equally`, `In parallel` |
| `Consequently` / `Thus` | `Hence`, `Therefore`, `As a result`, `It follows that`, `Accordingly` |
| `In sum` | `In short`, `Taken together`, `On balance`, `To summarize` |
| `In principle` | `In theory`, `Conceptually`, `Absent frictions`, `Under the model's assumptions` |
| `Following [Author]` | `Building on`, `Adapting`, `In the spirit of`, `Extending`, `As in` |
| `consistent with` | `aligns with`, `points to`, `supports`, `matches`, `accords with` |
| `Note that` / `It is worth noting that` | usually delete; if needed: `Importantly,` only when truly important |
| `the present document` / `the present paper` | `we`, `this paper`, or just delete the meta-reference |

### Words the author avoids

<!-- The writer should NOT use these ‚Äî the author has rejected them elsewhere. -->

[e.g., "delve", "leverage", "nuanced", "robust" (as an adjective), "comprehensive"]
These never appear in the corpus and should not be introduced ‚Äî they are AI-pattern markers: `delve`, `tapestry`, `myriad`, `plethora`, `navigate` (metaphorical), `unpack`, `realm`, `landscape` (metaphorical), `crucially` as sentence opener, `comprehensive`, `nuanced`, `seamlessly`, `cutting-edge`, `paradigm`, `bombshell`, `game-changer`, `at the heart of`, `lies at the intersection of`. Vague-verb `leverage` is also out; the technical sense ("leverage instrument variation") is fine.

### Hedging pattern

- **Hedge identification claims:** "consistent with", "may reflect", "is suggestive of"
- **Do not hedge point estimates or descriptive facts:** state them flat
- **Do not hedge literature summaries:** "Wei et al. (2023) find that EVs are over-subsidized" ‚Äî not "Wei et al. suggest that EVs may be over-subsidized" unless that is what they wrote.
- **Maximum one hedge per sentence.** Stacked hedges ("may possibly be likely to suggest") are out.

### Comparison pattern

By attribution and number, not by vague comparison: *"Our 7-pp estimate is roughly half the 14-pp effect reported by Smith (2018)."* Not *"larger than prior work"* or *"in line with the literature."*

## Citation Conventions

- Textual vs. parenthetical split: ~40% textual ("Smith (2018) finds‚Ä¶") in intro, ~70% parenthetical in results, 
- **Number of papers cited per claim: max 2 in introductions with a strong preference for 1, up to 3 in the lit review section.**
- Self-citations: sparse, only when building directly on prior work
- "et al." at 3+ authors; full author list at 2 authors

## Tone Markers

<!-- Qualitative signals the writer should reproduce. -->

- Confident without bombast ‚Äî state results flatly, flag limits frankly
- **No self-deprecation in main text**
- No overclaim
- No rhetorical questions in research papers
- Limitations paragraphs are long and frank
- No epigraphs in research-paper introductions (acceptable in essays and dissertations)

## Notes for the Writer Agent

<!-- Any global overrides to the defaults in writer.md and domain-profile.md. -->

- **Lexicon variety is a learning goal**, not a stylistic preference. Flag uses of lexicon variety in a comment so the author can verify ("‚Üê suggesting `accords with` instead of `consistent with`").
- **Sentence-rhythm variety is a goal too.** Do not let setup-clause openers exceed ~25% of a section.
- **Numbered contribution paragraphs are capped at 3 items.** If the writer feels compelled to list a fourth, it belongs in the body, not the intro.
- **Roadmap is one short sentence** in manuscript drafts; long roadmap permitted only in working-paper and dissertation drafts.
- **Headline number must appear by paragraph 2** of any introduction.
- **Use "column (1)" not "specification (1)"** in tables (project convention from `domain-profile.md`).
- **Use "standard error" not "SE"** in prose; "s.e." acceptable in table notes.
- **First person:** "we" in joint work; "I" only in solo memos describing design choices; avoid first person in result reporting.
- **In Spanish drafts:** prefer active voice with a clear agent over `se`-passives where the agent is recoverable.
- **Limitations paragraph is mandatory.**
- **Hedging budget:** at most one hedge per sentence; reserve hedges for identification and external validity, not point estimates or descriptive facts.
